Lesson 1 · God and Revelation

The God question

Before asking whether a proof works, ask why human beings keep returning to the question at all.

Lesson aim: distinguish natural knowledge of God from divine revelation, and explain why Catholic faith does not ask reason to commit suicide.
1 · Encounter

A question hiding in plain sight

Human beings do not merely survive. They ask whether life means anything, whether goodness is real, why anything exists, and whether death has the last word. Those questions appear in philosophy, science, art, grief, love and ordinary conversation long before they appear in a catechism.

Write before reading further:
  1. What do you presently mean by the word God?
  2. What could reason discover without a Bible or Church?
  3. What would require God freely to reveal it?
2 · Primary reading

Read Deharbe slowly

Primary historical text:

Joseph Deharbe, A Complete Catechism of the Catholic Religion, First Article, section 1, questions 3–8. Read the assigned section.

Controlling modern reference: Catechism of the Catholic Church, paragraphs 27–49.

Scripture: Psalm 19:1–4; Wisdom 13:1–9; Romans 1:19–21; Acts 17:22–28.

As you read, mark every claim about what reason can know, every claim about revelation, and every place where the author assumes vocabulary a modern reader may not possess.

Deharbe supplies the historical route through the question. The modern Catechism supplies the authoritative framework within which that route is interpreted.
3 · Key distinction

Reason is not revelation

Natural knowledge

Reason can move from the world, causality, order, contingency, moral experience and the human desire for truth toward knowledge that God exists.

Divine revelation

God freely discloses himself and his saving purpose. The Trinity and the mystery of Christ are not deductions from astronomy or ethics.

Faith

Faith is the human response, enabled by grace, to God who reveals. It involves intellect, will, trust and the whole person.

4 · Test the claim

Three objections

  1. “Science has explained God away.” Does explaining a process answer why there is a reality capable of process at all?
  2. “Faith means believing without evidence.” Is that the Catholic account of faith, or a modern caricature?
  3. “If reason can know God, revelation is unnecessary.” Does knowing that someone exists amount to knowing that person’s inner life and purpose?
Label your conclusions: Which statements are philosophical arguments, which are magisterial teaching, and which remain open to debate about the strength of a particular proof?
5 · Write and demonstrate

Make the learning visible

Notebook task

Define natural knowledge, revelation and faith in your own words.

Draw a three-part diagram showing their relationship.

Record one objection you still find serious.

Demonstration

Without notes, explain in two minutes why Catholicism does not present faith and reason as enemies.

Then write one paragraph correcting anything your spoken explanation missed.

Further reading — optional:

For an Irish adult companion, see the Irish Catholic Catechism for Adults. For a British introduction, see the CTS WHY? Course, beginning with “Why God?”. These are companions, not replacements for Deharbe or the Catechism.
