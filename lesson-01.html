Lesson 1 · God and Revelation

The God question

Work through one stage at a time. Open the next section only when you have completed the present task.

Lesson aim: distinguish natural knowledge of God from divine revelation, and explain why the Catholic Church does not ask reason to compete with revelation.
1
Encounter

Write your thoughts before reading

Begin without looking anything up. Record your genuine starting point rather than trying to produce the correct Catholic answer immediately.

Write briefly:
  1. What do you presently mean by the word God?
  2. Why do human beings keep asking whether God exists?
  3. What might reason discover on its own?
  4. What might require God freely to reveal it?
2
Primary reading

Read Joseph Deharbe

Read: Joseph Deharbe, A Complete Catechism of the Catholic Religion, First Article, section 1, questions 3–8.

Open the assigned Deharbe reading →

Using a physical copy? Find First Article, §1, questions 3–8. Page numbers vary between editions.

First reading: read slowly from beginning to end without stopping to solve every difficulty. Try to grasp the direction of the argument.
3
Read again and notice

Take guided notes, one section at a time

Read the passage again in short sections—usually one question and answer, or one paragraph, at a time. After each section, pause and write four brief lines.

1. ClaimWhat is the author saying?

Write one sentence in your own words. Do not copy unless a phrase is especially important.

2. ReasonWhy does he say it?

Record the example, argument, distinction or evidence he uses.

3. MeaningWhy does it matter?

State what this changes or clarifies about the Catholic understanding of God.

4. QuestionWhat remains unclear?

Write one doubt, objection or point that needs checking.

Vocabulary line: beneath those four notes, list any unfamiliar word, dated expression or theological term. Underline it in a physical copy or write it exactly as it appears online. Look it up only after you have finished that short section.
Keep each set compact.

A useful target is four to six handwritten lines per section. The purpose is to reconstruct the argument, not transcribe the book.

At the end of the whole reading, add:
  • a five-sentence summary of Deharbe’s overall argument;
  • the strongest claim in the passage;
  • the claim you find least convincing or least clear;
  • your vocabulary list.
4
Key distinction

Reason is not revelation

Before consulting the modern sources, make the central distinction visible.

Natural knowledge

Reason can move from creation, causality, contingency, order, moral experience and the desire for truth towards genuine knowledge that God exists.

Divine revelation

God freely discloses himself and his saving purpose. The Trinity and the mystery of Christ cannot be deduced merely from nature.

Faith

Faith is the grace-enabled human response to God who reveals, involving intellect, will, trust and the whole person.

Why this is more surprising than it first appears: Catholic thought does not begin by asking reason to stand aside. It claims that reason can genuinely reach towards God, while also insisting that knowing God exists is not the same as knowing who God is or what God intends.
Write one sentence for each term without copying the wording above. Then draw arrows showing how reason, revelation and faith relate without collapsing into one another.
5
Controlling modern reference

Read the Catechism and the Psalms

Read: Catechism of the Catholic Church, paragraphs 27–49.

Then read: Psalm 19:1–4 and Psalm 8.

Optional supporting texts: Wisdom 13:1–9; Romans 1:19–21; Acts 17:22–28.

The modern Catechism is the authoritative reference for how the Church presents this teaching today.

The substance has not changed: the Church still teaches that human reason can reach genuine knowledge of God from creation, while revelation draws humanity into knowledge and communion that reason alone could not attain.

The modern Catechism expresses the same doctrine with unusual beauty: the desire for God is written in the human heart, creation bears witness to its Creator, and reason remains capable of recognising that witness.
Compare the sources: note one point where Deharbe and the modern Catechism clearly agree, one place where the Catechism expresses the teaching more fully or beautifully, and one image from the Psalms that makes the idea memorable.
6
Test the claim

Face three objections

“Science has explained God away.”

Does explaining how a process works answer why there is a reality capable of process at all?

“Faith means believing without evidence.”

Is that the Catholic account of faith, or a modern caricature of it?

“If reason can know God, revelation is unnecessary.”

Does knowing that someone exists amount to knowing that person’s inner life and purpose?

Choose the strongest objection. First explain why an intelligent person might find it persuasive. Then answer it using the distinctions and sources from this lesson.
7
Write and demonstrate

Make the learning visible

Notebook task

Define natural knowledge, revelation and faith in your own words.

Summarise Deharbe’s argument in no more than five sentences.

Record one objection you still find serious.

Demonstration

Without notes, explain in two minutes why Catholicism does not present faith and reason as enemies.

Then reopen your notes and write one paragraph correcting or improving what you said.

Before leaving the lesson

  1. Has this lesson helped you understand the Catholic faith more clearly? Why, or why not?
  2. What part of the text did you find most convincing or most difficult?
  3. Has your understanding changed since your starting notes?

Lesson 1

God and Revelation

A guided seven-stage lesson

Work in order

Complete one accordion before opening the next. Your notes need not be polished; they need to show what you understood, questioned and corrected.

Note pattern

Claim → Reason → Meaning → Question
Then add unfamiliar vocabulary.

Estimated time

60–75 minutes, preferably away from notifications.

Materials

Deharbe, the modern Catechism, Bible or Psalter, ruled notebook and pen.

Begin honestly, read carefully, distinguish clearly, and leave evidence of what changed.
