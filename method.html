The Notebook Method

The website should make study possible, not make thought unnecessary.

1

First encounter

Write what you presently think before consulting the explanatory material. This preserves the evidence of change.

2

Slow reading

Mark unfamiliar terms, central claims, assumptions and apparent objections. Do not highlight everything.

3

Closed-book recall

Shut the source and explain its argument in your own words. Gaps become visible immediately.

4

Return and correct

Compare your account with the text, Church sources and commentary. Corrections are part of the experiment.

One double-page spread

A notebook that records thinking

Use an ordinary ruled notebook. The value lies in the repeated structure, not expensive stationery.

Left page: evidence

Date and lesson

Vocabulary and definitions

Short quotations with exact references

Facts, dates and distinctions

Questions raised by the text

Right page: understanding

Explanation in my own words

What surprised or challenged me

Connections with previous lessons

What I had misunderstood

One sentence worth retaining

The review rhythm

After the lesson

Write a five-sentence account without looking back.

After one week

Reconstruct the lesson’s central distinction and answer its main objection.

After the module

Create a one-page map, ten key terms and three unresolved questions.

The object is not to finish pages. It is to become capable of explaining what the pages mean, where their authority comes from and how they connect.
Read. Write. Recall. Correct. Connect. Demonstrate.
