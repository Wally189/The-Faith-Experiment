Authority · context · copyright

How sources are used

An old Catholic book is a starting text, not a private substitute for the Church’s living teaching office.

Controlling authorities

  • Sacred Scripture
  • The Catechism of the Catholic Church
  • Ecumenical councils and papal teaching
  • The current Code of Canon Law where law is relevant

Historical witnesses

  • Fathers and Doctors of the Church
  • Historical catechisms and manuals
  • Liturgical and devotional sources
  • Church historians and theologians

Modern checks

  • Current scholarship and historical evidence
  • Changed terminology and disciplines
  • British, Irish and European pastoral context
  • Areas of genuine theological dispute

The distinction displayed in every lesson

Dogma truth revealed by God and definitively proposed as such.

Doctrine authoritative teaching requiring the appropriate assent.

Discipline ecclesiastical law or practice that may change.

Theological opinion a reasoned position that Catholics may sometimes debate.

Historical context assumptions, knowledge or language belonging to the author’s period.

Copyright rule

The project may reproduce only text that is genuinely public domain or expressly licensed for that use. A modern translation can remain protected even when the ancient original is not. Current Church documents and modern books will normally be linked, cited and discussed rather than copied wholesale.

Editorial promise: every foundational text will receive an edition-specific rights check before a clean transcription is published.
The age of a text establishes neither its copyright status nor its doctrinal authority.
