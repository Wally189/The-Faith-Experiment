Your notebook

A simple four-stage method

You do not need polished academic notes. You need enough evidence to see what you thought before reading, what the sources actually said, and what changed afterwards.

1 · Before

Record your starting point

Answer the lesson’s opening questions without searching for the approved answer. Include assumptions, doubts and experiences that shape your present view.

2 · During

Reconstruct the argument

For each short section use Claim → Reason → Meaning → Question. Add unfamiliar vocabulary separately rather than allowing one difficult word to halt the whole reading.

3 · After

State what you now think

Write a brief summary in your own words. Note what became clearer, what you changed your mind about, and what remains unresolved.

4 · Revisit

Check what survived

Return later without the text. Explain the central distinction aloud or in writing, then correct the gaps using your notes and the controlling sources.

When using a physical book

Mark lightly and consistently. The aim is to make a later rereading quicker, not to colour every line.

Underline
Main claim or definition.
Vertical line in margin
A longer passage containing one argument.
?
Something doubtful or unclear.
V
Vocabulary or dated wording to check.

Write your fuller response in the notebook; keep marks in the book brief enough that the original text remains readable.
